## Long body (4000 chars max)

If you have ever looked at a wargame and wondered how someone made it work, this book is for you.

Wargame design can look harder than it is from the outside. The maps are dense. The counters are full of numbers. The rules can run for dozens of pages. But under all of that, a good wargame is built from a series of understandable decisions: what the game is about, what the players can do, what the system needs to model, and what can be left out.

*A Contemporary Guide to Wargame Design* is a practical guide to making those decisions. Ray Weiss draws on more than a decade of designing and publishing historical board wargames to explain how a subject becomes a playable design. The book covers the full path from first idea to finished prototype, with attention to the parts new designers usually find confusing: research, scope, scale, orders of battle, movement, combat systems, sequence of play, supply, morale, victory conditions, playtesting, rules writing, development, and publication.

This is not a book about theory for its own sake. It is about the work of turning history into a game players can sit down and play. Why does one combat results table feel right while another feels arbitrary? How do you decide whether a hex should represent 500 meters or 50 kilometers? When should a unit have one combat value, and when does it need separate ratings for attack, defense, morale, or movement? How do you know when a clever subsystem is helping the design, and when it is just adding work for the player?

The answers are not universal. A tactical game about infantry combat needs different tools than an operational game about supply lines, and a card-driven game needs different habits than a classic hex-and-counter design. The point is not to give every designer the same recipe. The point is to show how experienced designers think through problems, test assumptions, and cut away anything that does not serve the game.

This book is written for hobbyist wargamers who want to design their first game. It is also useful for board game designers curious about conflict simulation, professional wargamers who want to understand the craft of commercial design, and experienced designers who have a project stuck on the table.

You do not need a publishing contract, a warehouse, or a giant art budget to start. You need a subject you care about, enough research to understand it, and a design small enough to finish. This book shows you how to get from there to a working wargame.
