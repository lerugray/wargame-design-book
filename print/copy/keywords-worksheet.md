# Keywords and BISAC Worksheet

## Positioning Notes

The closest Amazon discovery paths are split across three shelves: wargame history and design, board game design, and tabletop game design. The true wargame design shelf is small. Jim Dunnigan's design books are foundational but older, Rick Priestley and John Lambshead's *Tabletop Wargames* sits closer to miniatures and hobby writing, and broader board game design books capture more search traffic. The right strategy is to claim the wargame niche directly while borrowing the larger "board game design" and "tabletop game design" language where it fits.

## Candidate Amazon Keywords

1. **wargame design**: The most direct phrase for readers who already know they want a book about designing conflict simulations.
2. **war game design**: Captures the spaced spelling used by readers who are not deep enough in the hobby to search "wargame."
3. **designing wargames**: Targets the action phrase a first-time designer is likely to type after deciding to make a game.
4. **board game design**: Reaches the larger design audience that may not identify as wargamers yet.
5. **tabletop game design**: Catches hobby designers coming from RPGs, miniatures, and modern board games.
6. **conflict simulation**: Speaks to professional wargamers, military analysts, and the older SPI school of design.
7. **historical board games**: Targets readers who approach the subject from history and want games grounded in real events.
8. **hex and counter wargames**: Reaches grognards looking for classic board wargame craft.
9. **military strategy games**: Bridges the military history and strategy-game audiences without promising a general strategy guide.
10. **game mechanics**: Pulls in designers looking for help with systems, not only theme or publishing advice.
11. **combat results table**: A narrow phrase, but high intent for hex-and-counter designers working on combat systems.
12. **playtesting board games**: Targets readers stuck between prototype and finished design.
13. **rules writing for games**: Finds designers who have mechanics but need to explain them clearly.
14. **professional wargaming**: Reaches defense, education, and analysis readers who want commercial design lessons.
15. **simulation games**: Captures a broader search path for people interested in modeling real systems through play.

## Candidate BISAC Categories

1. **GAMES & ACTIVITIES / Board Games (GAM001000)**: Best primary shelf because the book is about designing physical board wargames.
2. **DESIGN / Games (DES016000)**: Strong fit for the craft angle and useful for readers browsing game design books rather than hobby-game books.
3. **HISTORY / Military / Strategy (HIS027060)**: Good secondary discovery path for readers who approach wargames through military history and operational problems.
4. **TECHNOLOGY & ENGINEERING / Military Science (TEC025000)**: Useful wildcard for professional wargaming, defense education, and conflict-simulation readers.
5. **GAMES & ACTIVITIES / General (GAM000000)**: A fallback if KDP's category picker does not expose the more useful board-game shelf cleanly.
6. **GAMES & ACTIVITIES / Role Playing & Fantasy (GAM010000)**: Not a true match, but worth considering only if Amazon's tabletop browse paths connect it to adjacent hobby designers.
