## Case Study: *Veridian Contraption*

Most of my published games are hex-and-counter operational simulations. *Sedan 1940*, *1916*, *Rostov '41*. The catalog runs on hexes, CRTs, and movement points. *Veridian Contraption* is not a tabletop game at all.

It is an autonomous emergent world-simulation that runs inside a terminal. There is no board and there are no counters. The interface is a colored ASCII map on the left and a scrolling narrative log on the right. Settlements register as text blocks, trade routes advance by coordinate, and the log chronicles the world in dense, register-shifting prose. The simulation runs whether I am active or idle. Civilizations rise, fracture, and reorganize while the program sits in the background, and returning to it means reading the consequences of a system that did not pause for the designer.

Underneath the presentation, the operational grammar remains intact. Combat still resolves on a combat results table. Settlements raise levies, cavalry, and artillery. Towns construct walls and defend behind them in sieges. Foreign powers approach from off the map in fixed tiers: rumor, then sighting, then arrival. When they land, they invade, negotiate, or break apart. The mechanics of traditional wargaming run underneath a procedural system rather than across a printed map.

None of this resembles hex-and-counter. There is no board to maneuver on, no cardboard stack, no terrain chart to consult, and, deliberately, no rendered graphics. I chose the terminal because the subject demanded it. The subject is a living world that must be mechanically deep before it is allowed to be visually coherent. Graphics are a resource sink. Every hour spent making a settlement look like a settlement is an hour not spent on the logic that makes the settlement behave. The terminal removes that temptation and forces the entire development budget into the simulation. A prettier *Veridian Contraption* would have been a mechanically shallower one.

This is the argument of the chapter extended past the hex grid. The format should serve the subject. When the subject is a fight for a river crossing, hexes and a CRT are the correct instruments, and I reach for them without hesitation. When the subject is a single besieged city, I reach for dice pools and resource tracks. When the subject is a whole world that requires systemic depth before visual polish, I reach for a terminal and pour everything into the simulation. The medium is a tool, not a constraint.
