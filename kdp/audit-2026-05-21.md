# WDB Audit — Consolidated Findings (2026-05-21)

First full editorial pass on *A Contemporary Guide to Wargame Design* (23 chapters,
6 appendices, ~70k words). Produced by a 5-agent audit: four chunk-auditors
(ch 1–6 / 7–12 / 13–18 / 19–23+appendices), each running historical-accuracy +
line-edit + within-chapter redundancy passes; one whole-book agent for
cross-chapter redundancy and developmental structure.

**Scope:** audited the print source `docs/_chapters/`. **All fixes must be applied
to BOTH `docs/_chapters/chapter_NN.md` (print) and `docs/chapter_NN.md` (website)** —
the two copies have diverged. This document is the canonical record; the per-agent
transcripts are not persisted.

**Status:** AUDIT ONLY — nothing has been edited. This is a findings report for Ray
to act on.

---

## Bottom line

The book is in **good shape** for a manuscript that has never had an editing pass —
structurally coherent, consistent voice, terminology impressively disciplined, and
the overwhelming majority of factual claims verified correct. The audit found
**5 hard factual errors**, ~3 facts to verify/loosen, 4 soft/debatable claims,
**one genuine structural editorial defect** (a dangling reference in Ch 18), a
pervasive redundancy pattern driven by the chapter-ending case-study template, and
**one real content gap** (no solitaire/solo-design treatment). Everything else is
polish: copy-edit cleanup and redundancy trims.

## Tier 1 — Factual errors (fix before next printing)

**1.1 — Ch 1 — Kriegsspiel / Jena chronology.**
Quoted: *"After the Prussian defeat at Jena during the Napoleonic Wars, the Prussian
general staff developed Kriegsspiel..."*
Wrong: Kriegsspiel was not a post-Jena general-staff development. It was invented by
Baron von Reisswitz (elder, ~1811–12) and refined by his son (~1819–24); the army
formally adopted the son's version in 1824. Jena (1806) drove the Scharnhorst
reforms and the Kriegsakademie (1810); Kriegsspiel postdates those.
Fix: decouple the two — Scharnhorst's reforms rebuilt the officer corps after Jena;
*then* Reisswitz introduced Kriegsspiel; the army adopted it in 1824.

**1.2 — Ch 6 (Sources list) — Nafziger *Imperial Bayonets* publisher.**
Quoted: *"Nafziger, George F. Imperial Bayonets. Novato: Presidio Press, 1996."*
Wrong publisher. *Imperial Bayonets* (1996) was Greenhill Books (London) / Stackpole
(US). Presidio Press was a real Novato military-history publisher — the likely source
of the confusion — but did not publish this title.
Fix: → *"London: Greenhill Books, 1996."*

**1.3 — Ch 9 — SPI publication name.**
Quoted: *"Richard Berg wrote a piece on the subject in SPI's Special Study #2..."*
Wrong: the SPI publication is **Staff Study #2** — *Wargame Design* (SPI, 1977).
Fix: "Special Study #2" → "Staff Study #2".

**1.4 — Ch 11 — *Der Weltkrieg* publisher.**
Quoted: *"Der Weltkrieg, the massive World War I strategic series published by Rand
Game Associates..."*
Wrong: *Der Weltkrieg* is published by **SPW (Schroeder Publishing & Wargames)**,
designed by David Schroeder. Rand Game Associates was an unrelated 1974–76 publisher
that folded long before. Also: the series is generally described as **operational**,
not "strategic" (minor — Ray's call).
Fix: → "published by Schroeder Publishing & Wargames (SPW)."

**1.5 — Appendix D — Davout's march to Austerlitz.**
Quoted: *"Davout's III Corps marched roughly seventy miles in forty-six hours to
reach Austerlitz on December 1, 1805..."*
Wrong: the march took **48 hours**, not 46 (struck camp dawn 30 Nov 1805, arrived
late 1 Dec; ~70 mi / 112 km in 48 hrs). "~70 miles" and "December 1, 1805" are correct.
Fix: "forty-six hours" → "forty-eight hours".

## Tier 2 — Facts to verify / loosen

**2.1 — Appendix D — Roman march-rate labels conflict with Vegetius.**
The Roman Legions table labels *iter justum* "15 Roman miles" and *iter magnum*
"20 Roman miles." Vegetius gives the common military step as 20 Roman miles and the
full step as 24. The modern-mile estimates (~14 / ~18 mi/day) are reasonable for
*sustained* marching, but the Roman-mile parentheticals are one tier low.
Fix: correct the Roman-mile column to 20 / 24, OR add a half-sentence noting
Vegetius's figures are training-pace standards while the table reports realistic
sustained daily rates (cite Roth, *Logistics of the Roman Army*).

**2.2 — Ch 14 — COIN "fifteen volumes" (likely overcount).**
Quoted (twice): *"Fifteen volumes and counting..."* Published COIN volumes are closer
to twelve (through *Red Dust Rebellion*). "Fifteen" is reachable only by counting
P500/in-development titles.
Fix: loosen to "more than a dozen volumes and counting" (the argument doesn't need
the exact figure), or verify against the GMT COIN page.

**2.3 — Ch 14 — Andean Abyss deck construction is imprecise.**
Quoted: *"The fourth Propaganda Card gets the remaining event cards."* The 76-card
total and the three stacks of 16 are correct, but the procedure as written omits
steps (two extra event cards on top, set-aside order) and reads like an exact
instruction.
Fix: soften — "...with the fourth Propaganda Card seeded into the remaining event
cards" — so it doesn't read as a how-to.

## Tier 3 — Soft / debatable claims (Ray's judgment)

- **Ch 7** — *"The Prussians destroyed and conquered it in less than three months."*
  Defensible for the *Imperial* French army (Sedan 1 Sep, Metz 27 Oct) but the war
  ran to Jan 1871. Suggest tightening to the army, not the country.
- **Ch 7** — *"The French army was highly regarded by most of Europe in 1870."*
  Overstates the consensus; Königgrätz (1866) had already shifted European opinion
  toward Prussia. Suggest "still carried a formidable reputation, built on the
  Crimea and Italy."
- **Ch 10** — Panzer Campaigns described as having a *"fluid turn structure."* It is
  IGO-UGO; what's fluid is the in-turn activation order. Suggest "the fluidity of
  its activations."
- **Ch 21** — Van Riper *"resigned his command... in protest."* He stepped down to
  an advisory role rather than leaving the exercise. Defensible as written; optional
  precision: "stepped down as commander of the opposing force in protest."

**Logged as verified CORRECT:** the Ch 11 *"Italian front in 1866"* reference — the
Third Italian War of Independence (1866) did require Italy to attack Austrian
fortresses in Venetia for political reasons. This is **not** an error. (The vast
majority of checked facts — designers, games, publishers, dates, OOBs, the Tunisia
and Millennium Challenge material, Appendix E's biographies — verified accurate.)

## Tier 4 — Editorial defects (clear errors to fix)

- **Ch 18 — dangling reference.** *"...all three of those things have happened to
  me..."* — the three things are not enumerated before this sentence (they appear
  later). The one genuine structural editorial defect. Fix: reword to "every one of
  those failures" or move the sentence after the list.
- **Ch 20 — parallel-structure fault.** *"...if your price does not cover production,
  shipping, platform fees, and leave enough margin..."* — "and leave" breaks the
  parallel. Fix: "...fails to cover production, shipping, and platform fees while
  leaving enough margin..."
- **Ch 17 — "less" vs "fewer."** *"My later ones generated less [errata]."* — errata
  is countable. Fix: "fewer."
- **Ch 13 — questions punctuated as statements.** e.g. *"...where do the shipping
  lanes run."* — needs a question mark.
- **Ch 15 — sentence fragments (no main verb).** The *"Looking at a map of the
  campaign area..."* fragment and the *"Seeing the specific formations involved..."*
  fragment both need a verb or attachment to the adjacent sentence.
- **Ch 3 — typo.** *"no such a thing as too much research"* → "no such thing."

**Minor line-edits (clustered, located — apply during a copy-edit pass):** run-ons /
buried points flagged in Ch 1 (¶ on Russian 1914/2022 — drop perishable "recently"),
Ch 2 ("childless except for a cat" clause order; "basically" twice), Ch 5 (river
hexside run-on; "prettying things up"), Ch 6 (introduce "SP" on first use), Ch 11
(200-word supply paragraph should split; "it" with no antecedent), Ch 13 (naval-combat

## Tier 5 — Within-chapter redundancy (recommended trims)

Heaviest first:

- **Ch 5 — biggest in the book's first half.** "From Playtest Map to Published Map"

  redo; East Prussia map) back-to-back in full. Cut one telling or make the Case
  Study a tight synthesis. Also: "hex = operational space, not a literal container"
  3×; the Peloponnesian $500–600 map anecdote twice.
- **Ch 8 — "When to Leave It Out" section nearly entirely duplicates "Choosing What
  to Include"** (the scale-by-scale facing breakdown — near-verbatim in places).
  Recommend cutting the section, folding its one fresh point (guerrilla fighters)
  into the 20th-century entry. Also: "ASL omits facing" 2–3×.
- **Ch 16 — "Finding Playtesters": "recruit critics, not praise" stated 3× in
  consecutive paragraphs.** Collapse to two.
- **Ch 14 — the hand-size-as-morale mechanic (borrowed from *Hitler's Reich*)
  explained 3×** (Deck-Building section / Granular Subsystems / GNW Case Study).
  Keep the full version in the Case Study; make the others one-line pointers.
- **Ch 20 — "design skill ≠ business skill" stated 5+×.** Keep the bookends and the
  vivid "a designer playing at being a publisher"; trim the rest.
- **Ch 18 — "fifteen minutes of verification" 3–4×**, and the verification checklist
  spelled out twice. Keep the abstract checklist once + the concrete case-study once.
- **Ch 12 — "Scaled Victory" and "Designing for Lopsided History" overlap** — both

- **Ch 4 — "tactical games look simpler than they are" argued 3×**; PCS air-rules

- **Ch 10 — "every phase runs every turn, cut dead weight" 4×**; "SOP is a program

- **Ch 11 — "include what the simulation demands" 4+×**; the "Integration and

- Lighter: Ch 1 (narrative-produces-wargames point doubled), Ch 2 (Sedan "foregone

## Tier 6 — Cross-chapter redundancy: the case-study problem

~18 of 23 chapters end with a "Case Study" section. Because Ray's catalog is finite,
the same dozen games get re-narrated across many chapters — the book's single biggest
redundancy engine. Repeated full narratives:

- **We Were Not Cowards / Sedan-1870 "foregone conclusion"** — ch 2, 12, 15 (echoed
  4, 5, 7). ~4 full tellings. Canonical home: Ch 2 (origin) + Ch 12 (lopsided-victory
  design). Others → cross-references.
- **Jack Greene shell-weight naval lesson** — ch 6, 7, 13 (3×). Home: Ch 13.
- **PCS air-rules BGG flowchart** — ch 4, 13 (3× counting echoes); **PCS chit/UQR
  system** — ch 6, 9 (3×). Home: Ch 13 / Ch 9 respectively.
- **1987: On to Kaliningrad / 1968: Tet discontinuation** — ch 2, 7 (Ch 16 cross-refs
  correctly).
- **Königgrätz / Mars-la-Tour March↔Battle formation flip** — ch 7, 8 (+ glossary).
- **"80% of design is plagiarism"** — ch 3, 9 (3×).
- **Kevin Zucker's chrome-skepticism** — recurring refrain ch 4, 5, 9, 10, 11
  (partly deliberate motif — Zucker is the stated mentor).
- **Simonsen rivers-on-hexsides / physical-systems design** — Ch 5 section vs
  Appendix E entry tell the same story in depth. Decide which is canonical.

Recommendation: don't tear out the case studies — they're valuable — but (a) make
each foreground the *one* aspect relevant to its chapter and defer the rest with
cross-references, and (b) consider replacing 1–2 with examples from *other designers'*
games to relieve catalog fatigue.

## Tier 7 — Structural / developmental

The arc works; the 5-part structure is mostly sound; the book delivers on its Ch 1
promises. Issues, in priority order:

- **Content gap: solitaire / solo design.** No chapter, no concentrated treatment —
  conspicuous for a *contemporary* guide. AMFIOG is solo-first; Appendix E profiles
  solo designers; the glossary defines solo play. Solo *design* (bot logic, AI-opponent
  decision tables, solo-first vs. solo-variant, replayability) is only mentioned in
  passing. The clearest gap — could be a Part III chapter or a major section of ch 14/21.
- **Part III is the weak division.** Only 2 chapters (13 Naval/Air, 14 CDG), unrelated
  to each other, and Ch 21 ("Beyond Hex and Counter," in Part V) duplicates its
  "beyond the hex grid" thesis. Consider merging/regrouping.
- **Depth imbalance.** Ch 14 is monograph-length (the longest); Ch 11 is overloaded
  (six subsystems in one chapter — supply alone deserves more room); Ch 4 (Scale) is
  thin for a decision the book repeatedly calls foundational; Ch 13's air half is
  thinner than its naval half (openly disclaimed).
- **Ordering — one real miss:** ZOC is used substantively in Ch 5–6 before Ch 7
  introduces it. Gloss it at first use in Ch 5. (CRT/DRM before Ch 9 and
  disruption/demoralization before Ch 11 are lower severity — glossary covers them.)
- **Lesser gaps:** balance as a topic is scattered across ch 12/16/19, never
  centralized; scenario design has no dedicated treatment; physical-production
  specifics are fragmented across ch 18/20/Appendix B.
- **Ch 1 over-promises** a professional-wargaming audience (defense analysts,
  educators) that the hobby-design-focused body doesn't fully serve. Trim the Ch 1
  claim or acknowledge the book is hobby-design-first.

## Tier 8 — Global consistency passes (book-wide)

- **Number style** — spelled-out vs numeral is mixed ("five and nine" but "72",
  "4", "55"). Adopt one rule (e.g. spell 1–9, numerals for 10+ and component
  counts/stats) and apply book-wide. Currency too ("$15" vs "fifteen dollars").
- **Game titles** — currently plain roman everywhere; standard convention italicizes
  published game titles. Pervasive. Pick a rule for game titles and one for
  book/magazine titles; enforce.
- **"hex-and-counter"** — body usage is hyphenated (correct as a compound modifier);
  the Ch 21 title "Beyond Hex and Counter" is not. Make the body uniform.
- **"Eurogame" vs "euro game"** — Ch 16 "Eurogame" vs Ch 14 "euro game." Standardize
  on "Eurogame."
- **Card-type-name capitalization** — "Propaganda Cards" (cap) vs "event cards" (lc)
  inconsistently; "Fog of war cards" half-capped; AMFIOG "fate cards" vs "Fate cards"
  (ch 22 vs ch 21). Match each game's source rulebook, per-game.
- **"PCS" — "Procedural Combat System" vs "Procedural Combat Series"** used
  interchangeably for the same thing (ch 4/9 "System", ch 6/13 "Series"). Pick the
  canonical name and apply it everywhere.
- **"DAMOS"** — established loosely ("Destroy All Monsters trilogy" in Ch 2, "DAMOS"
  later). Define the acronym clearly at first use so later references land.
- **"Imperial Bayonets"** — Ray's game series shares its name with Nafziger's
  reference book. A clarifying parenthetical at first game-series use prevents
  reader conflation.
- **Place-name accents** — "Koniggratz" / "Mars La Tour" in running prose should be
  Königgrätz / Mars-la-Tour. If the *game titles* deliberately drop accents, keep the
  titles but make running-prose place names correct, and be consistent.
- **"OOB" / "orders of battle" / "OOBs"** — spell out on first use per chapter,
  abbreviate after; standardize the plural "OOBs."
- **VASSAL cross-references disagree** — glossary says Ch 16,18; Appendix B says
  Ch 14,20. Reconcile.
- **en-dash for numeric ranges** — Appendix D uses hyphens; typesetting convention
  is the en-dash.
- **Howard *Franco-Prussian War* publisher** — Appendix A cites Routledge (current
  reprint), Appendix D cites Rupert Hart-Davis (1961 original). Both correct; pick a
  convention.
